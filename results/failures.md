# Failure Analysis

System analysed: the chosen operating point — **hybrid linear fusion (α=0.3),
bge-small (ONNX) + bm25s**. A "failure" here = the gold passage does not appear in
the **top 5** retrieved.

Generated by `experiments/find_failures.py` over all 500 FiQA dev queries.

## Aggregate picture

| Bucket | Count | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Failures (gold not in top 5) | 203 / 500 | recall@5 ≈ 0.59 |
| ↳ **Hard misses** (gold not in top 50) | 104 | retrieval never surfaced it — a reranker can't fix these |
| ↳ **Ranking failures** (gold in ranks 6–50) | 99 | candidate *was* fetched but ranked too low |
| Failures on short queries (≤10 words) | 122 / 203 | short queries are over-represented in failures |

Two structural facts fall out of this:

- **The hard misses (104) cap the achievable quality.** They bound recall@50 at
  ~0.79 — even a perfect reranker over the 50 candidates could not rescue them.
  Closing this gap needs *better candidates* (higher CANDIDATE_K, a stronger
  encoder, or query expansion), not better fusion/ranking.
- **Short queries dominate failures** (60% of failures, vs 52% of all queries),
  consistent with the bench.json stratification: fewer terms → less signal.

Below are three representative cases, each a different failure mode.

---

## Case 1 — Entity/keyword trap (hard miss)

**Query (qid=77, 8 words):** *"Can the IRS freeze a business Bank account?"*
**Gold passage (rank):** not in top 50 (hard miss).

**Gold [551315]:**
> "If the business is legally separated and not commingled - they probably cannot.
> What they can do is put a lien on it ... and garnish your income. If the corporate
> veil is pierced ... then they can treat it as if it is your personal asset."

**Top-5 retrieved passages:**
1. `[78912]` 0.700 — "Not by you. Your bank might have to fill some reports for the IRS, but for the customer, nothing needs to be done..."
2. `[12435]` 0.516 — "Just like with IRS refunds issued in errors, after 3 years it's legally yours..."
3. `[323573]` 0.438 — "...your tenant now knows your bank information ... he can ... freeze [it] if he wins a judgement."
4. `[490831]` 0.427 — "Do not try to deposit piece wise ... The fear of having your bank account frozen..."
5. `[525200]` 0.425 — "I wouldn't do this. There is a chance that your check could get lost/misdirected..."

**Diagnosis.** The query is dense with salient entities — *IRS, freeze, bank
account* — and both retrievers anchor on them. Every top result shares those tokens
or their semantics but answers a *different* question (IRS reporting requirements,
depositing cash, a landlord freezing an account via lawsuit). The gold answers the
actual latent question — *can the IRS legally freeze a **business** account* — using
entirely different vocabulary (*legally separated, commingled, lien, corporate
veil*). Because the discriminating concept ("business vs personal liability shield")
never appears as surface terms, neither BM25 nor a 384-dim bi-encoder pulls it into
the top 50. Classic single-vector limitation: it matches the *topic* (IRS + bank),
not the *proposition*.

**Fix to try.** Cross-encoder reranker or multi-vector retrieval (ColBERT) to score
the proposition, not just the topic; or query expansion of the latent legal concept.
(Note: a reranker alone can't fix this one — the gold isn't in the candidate set, so
first-stage recall must improve first.)

## Case 2 — Terse, low-overlap gold buried under verbose neighbours (ranking failure)

**Query (qid=868, 10 words):** *"Employer no longer withholds, how do I self administer 401k"*
**Gold passage (rank):** 49.

**Gold [408724]:**
> "You can't be doing it yourself. Only your employer can do it. If the employer
> doesn't provide the option - switch employers. The only way for you to do it
> yourself is if you're the employer, i.e.: self-employed."

**Top-5 retrieved passages:**
1. `[392894]` 0.700 — "You can probably roll it over into the new company's 401k too, so just talk to your HR rep..."
2. `[195366]` 0.687 — "individual 401k accounts are supposed to be funded from self-employment earnings, not from rollovers..."
3. `[456526]` 0.586 — "You're confusing between 'individual' 401k (Solo-401k) ... and IRA..."
4. `[34959]` 0.585 — "...her new employer won't know how much was contributed from the old job..."
5. `[92941]` 0.550 — "Former 401(k) administrator here. 1. ... 'roll-over' your existing 401(k)..."

**Diagnosis.** The gold is the *correct and direct* answer ("you can't — only your
employer can"), but it is short, blunt, and lexically thin: beyond "employer" and
"401k" it shares little with the query, and semantically it *negates* the premise.
The corpus is full of long, keyword-rich passages about 401k rollovers and
solo-401ks that overlap heavily with the query's surface terms, so they crowd the
top ranks. Retrieval rewards surface/semantic similarity, but the most *useful*
answer here pushes back on the question — and contrarian, low-overlap answers are
systematically under-ranked. Fusion doesn't help: BM25 and dense agree on the wrong
(verbose, on-topic) passages.

**Fix to try.** A cross-encoder reranker that scores question–answer *entailment*
rather than similarity. The gold is at rank 49, inside the candidate set, so a
reranker over the top-50 could promote it.

## Case 3 — Conventional-answer bias / corpus prior (ranking failure)

**Query (qid=10048, 7 words):** *"Higher mortgage to increase savings to invest?"*
**Gold passage (rank):** 45.

**Gold [554814]:**
> "...the fundamental question you're asking is, 'If I can borrow money for a low
> cost, and if I think I can invest it and receive returns greater than that cost,
> should I do it?' It doesn't matter where that money comes from — a mortgage that's
> bigger than it needs to be, a credit card teaser rate, or a margin line..."

**Top-5 retrieved passages:**
1. `[423403]` 0.777 — "The more you put down now, the less money you are borrowing. 30yrs of interest adds up..."
2. `[429338]` 0.700 — "Every payment you make on your house ... increases your equity ... move additional savings into other long-term funds."
3. `[326542]` 0.624 — "Could you get a better return on that money by investing it ... rate should be more than the mortgage rate..."
4. `[560915]` 0.548 — (short fragment)
5. `[35388]` 0.420 — "You can't transfer mortgages ... refinance ... leverage yourself as far as possible while rates are low."

**Diagnosis.** The query's intent is *leverage* — deliberately taking a **bigger**
mortgage to free cash to invest. The top results return the popular opposite advice
("put more down", "pay off the house"), the dominant sentiment in a personal-finance
corpus. The gold expresses the exact leverage framing the user asked for but ranks
45th. The retriever faithfully reflects the *corpus prior* (the most common answer to
"mortgage vs invest") rather than the *query's specific stance*. Result #3 is
conceptually close (compare return vs mortgage rate) — the relevant region is found,
but the precise match is swamped by higher-frequency conventional answers.

**Fix to try.** Cross-encoder reranking, or MMR-style diversification over the
candidates to surface the stance-matching minority instead of near-duplicate
conventional answers.

## Cross-cutting patterns and what would help

| Failure mode | Root cause | Plausible fix |
|---|---|---|
| Entity/keyword trap (Case 1) | Matches topic, not proposition; discriminating concept absent from surface terms | Stronger first-stage recall (better encoder / higher CANDIDATE_K), query expansion, multi-vector (ColBERT) |
| Buried terse answer (Case 2) | Relevance ≠ surface similarity; short negating answers under-ranked | Cross-encoder reranker scoring entailment, not similarity |
| Conventional-answer bias (Case 3) | Retriever reflects corpus frequency over query stance | Reranker, or MMR diversification |
| Short-query failures (122/203) | Too little signal in ≤10-word queries | Query expansion / rewriting before retrieval |

**Highest-leverage single change:** a **cross-encoder reranker** over the top-50
candidates would directly target Cases 2 and 3 (ranking failures where the gold is
already in the candidate set — 99 queries). It cannot help the 104 hard misses
(Case 1), which need better first-stage recall. The catch is the 50ms CPU budget:
even a small cross-encoder adds ~30–50ms, so it is only viable with a GPU or a
relaxed latency budget (see DESIGN trade-offs).
